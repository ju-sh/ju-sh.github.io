Imagining the reader

Talk by Benjamin Pierce.

  • Paper's presentation should be such that a first year PhD student must be able to understand it.
  • Abstract is like an advertisement of the paper.

Before a presentation:

  • Read out your work aloud to yourself.
    • Gives you a different perspective than reading.
  • Find friends to read out to.
  • Ask related people. Including 'competition'.

Iterate. Have many passes.

Good writing is rewriting.

Most writers polish draft after draft.

  • Steven Pinker

From Strunk and White, Elements of style:

Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should contain no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short or that he avoid all details and treat his subjects in outline, but that every word tell.

Every word has to be there for a reason. Compress to remove unnecessary words.

An example:

In this section, we describe some
of the highlights of the research
area. We discuss some of the most
significant, elegant and useful

How not to give a great research talk

What to do:

  • No need of outline slides
    • ∵ it's like a forward reference.
  • No need to explan all details.
    • Choose important aspects only.
  • Explain what you did.
    • But not everything you did.
  • Use examples.
  • Same beamer theme is tiresome.
    • Try having a unique theme.
  • Get timing right.
    • Skipping visible slides isn't great.
  • Make the last slide the takeaway message instead of 'Thank you'.
    • This is the slide that should stay visible during Q&A. Let the message sink in.
