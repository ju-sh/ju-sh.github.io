I read this novel partly in French and partly in English. Helped me pick up a lot of French words. Can read a bit of French now!

The novel, set primarily in French Algeria, narrates the story of Jonas and Émilie, that progresses as the Algerian civil war rages on.

DONE Outline

Jonas lives with this father, mother and younger sister in a rural area of Algeria.

Zahra is verbally challenged and can neither speak or hear.

Issa, Jonas's father, is a farmer who is expecting a plentiful harvest from his fields soon and is happy that he would able to pay off his debts for which he mortgaged his ancestral lands.

Unfortunately a fire breaks out in his field one night and destroys all of his crops. The family is forced out of their home.

Issa decides to travel to the city - Oran, in search of a new life.

Jonas is forced to abondon his dog as the dog won't be able to join them.

When the family reaches Oran, Issa meets his elder brother Mahieddine (aka Mahi) and explains his situation.

Mahi arranges a living space for the family via a broker named Bliss in a slum named Jenane Jato.

The family is not familiar with city life and struggles to cope with it. Issa even ends up leaving Jonas in an unsafe neighbourhood once.

Issa sends off Jonas to live with Mahieddine when Issa ends up killing a thug (nicknamed El Moro) who stole a lot of his money.

Mahi and his wife Germaine adopts him. Germaine is of French descent. A Catholic married to the Muslim Mahi.

The film had led me to believe their families disowned them for the inter-religious marriage due to the conservative nature of society at the time. Turns out that Mahi's father actually agreed to it. Not sure about Germaine's family though.

Issa turns to alcoholism afterwards and goes missing. Jonas has no contact with his biological mother for years afterwards, and Germaine and Mahieddine becomes his parents in almost every way.

Jonas would attempt to find to find his mother and sister, years afterwards, but by then it is too late as they had moved to somewhere else.

Jonas would never see his mother and sister again.

Thus he loses all connection to his immediate biological family before coming off age.

Jonas befriends his neighbour Lucette, who is of the same age. He is surprised to know that Jérôme, Lucette's father, is an atheist.

Meanwhile, Mahi had been involved in Algerian nationalist movements (even having once hosted Messali Hadj in his home for dinner once) and gets the attention of the police. Mahi spends a week in police custody and is devastated upon his release. He fears that his friends would isolate him thinking that he spilled the beans on them and decides to leave Oran for Rio Salado, which is around 60km away.

Jonas and Lucette are sad to part, but seem to accepts that there is nothing that they can do about it.

So the family moves from the city to a quiter country side. Having lived outside the city before joining Mahi, Jonas likes Rio Salado.

Mahi sets up a pharmacy with Germaine, where he first meets Émilie. Jonas notices her 'coal-black eyes'. Eyes dark as the night.

Jonas becomes friends with Isabelle. Isabelle considers Jonas her 'fiancée' but comes to see him in a different light when she realizes that he is of Arab ancestry.

This would be Jonas's first encounter with racism. Though Isabelle is still a child, she is prejudiced against Arabs.

Jean-Christophe beats Jonas up at school in a bid to impress Isabelle. Jonas refuses to say who did it to both the teacher and Germaine.

Under pressure from Isabelle and probably out of gratitude for not ratting him out, Jean-Christophe apologizes and Jonas becomes part of their gang. A friendship that would run throught out their lives.

Fabrice, 3 months younger to Jonas, was an aspring writer who liked to read books and write poems. His mother was a rich widow who had business interests in Oran and Rio Salado. She was the only in town who drove a vehicle.

Simon, who lived closest to Jonas's house, was his best friend. Described as the life of every party. His father was financially not well-off.

Jean-Christophe was the son of a caretaker. Being Isabelle's boyfriend, he has a hard time.

Part of the extended gang were the Sosa cousins: André and José.

WWII is going on. France surrendered to Nazi Germany. British forces bomb the French naval base in Oran as part of Operation Catapult. An event seen by many among France as an act of backstabbing by an ally.

Jonas gets a glimpse of Oran after this raid when he goes with Germaine to attend the funeral of one her nephews who died in it.

While in Oran, Jonas goes back Jenane Jeto to meet his mother, not knowing that it would be the last time he would ever see her.

Rio Salado hears of the Allied forces landing in Oran as part of Operation Torch. American soldiers go around Oran like tourists and Dedé befriends some of them.

Jonas is shocked to find that one of his old neighbours has ended up in a brothel. This makes him want to visit Jenane Jato again to see how his mother and sister are faring. But much to his despair, he finds that they had left years ago.

Later on while on an excursion to the beach with his friends, Jonas happens to meet Madame Cazenave, who lived close to Rio Salado.

Afterwards, Germaine assigns Jonas to deliver an order that Madame Cazanave had placed at the pharmacy, who being eager to see Cazanave again, happily accepts it. But upon reaching the Cazanaves' house, Madame Cazanave ends up seducing Jonas. For Cazanave it was casual, but Jonas becomes infatuated with her for a while. He attempts to meet her again but is brought back to his senses when she makes it plain that she didn't want a relationship with Jonas.

The Second World War, which had been going on, ended. But around the same time protests demanding Algerian independence were violently suppressed and incidents like the Sétif massacre occurred (in Constantine, Algeria).

Mahi, who had a deep interest in Algerian nationalism, has a heart attack leading to hospitalization around this time.

André's had an employee named Jelloul, whom he treated very poorly due in most part to Arab ancestry. Jonas helps him when André throws him out after a lot of beatings and without pay.

When Jonas asks if got another job lined up, Jelloul smiles mysteriously. It is revealed later that Jelloul joins the LNF. The way André treated Jelloul influenced his decision.

Yet Jelloul ends up doing similar things during his militant life, leading Jonas to ask him:

N'est-ce pas l'humiliation qui t'a contraint à porter les armes ? Pourquoi l'exerces-tu à ton tour, aujourd'hui ?

Wasn't it the humiliation that forced you to take arms? Why are you doing that in your turn today?

Jellould had no reply.

Jelloul was hurt and could hardly walk when he turned up at Jonas's place. So Jonas drops him off on his bicycle. Jonas is shocked to see the condition in which Jelloul's family lived.

Jonas says:

Je pensais avoir touché le fond de la misère, à Jenane Jato ; je me trompais. La misère du douar où habitait Jelloul et sa famille dépassait les bornes.

I thought I had touched the bottom of poverty at Jenane Jato; I was mistaken. The poverty of the douar where Jelloul and his family lived surpassed that.

(I suppose douar is a kind of slum.)

Turns out that the native Arabs live away from the town in Rio Salado. Jonas is afraid when he remembers that he is on the 'other side'. And that if anything happened to him, noone is going to look for him there. Thus indicating that the town-folk didn't care about the poorer people like Jelloul.

Jonas saw pot-bellied children at Jelloul's neighbourhood. Kwashiorkor. A sign of severe malnutrision.

The settlers brought development to Algeria, but they trampled on those who were there before them.

If the way André treats Jelloul is anything to go by, the colons treated the native Arabs like sub-humans.

It seems that many in the European part of the town didn't even know about the conditions in which people like Jelloul lived. Jonas was surprised at the poverty he saw.

As Jelloul said, Jonas was in a different situation.

Tu ne peux pas comprendre, toi. Tu es des nôtres, mais tu mènes leur vie…

You can't understand… You're one of us, but you lead their life.

The Algerian war was beginning, but Jonas and his friends didn't bother to pay it much attention as it doesn't disturb their life.. yet.

After the heart attack, Mahi couldn't be the man he used to be. He slowly began to appear to be losing his mind.

Sometime after the end of the Second World War, André opens an American style bar in the outskirts of Rio Salado and has a grand opening.

The entire gang is captivated by a girl who comes to attend the opening of the bar.

She was Madame Cazanave's daughter, Émilie. She quickly becomes Fabrice's girlfriend. Jonas doesn't realize at this point that this is the same Émilie that he used to know in his childhood.

But Émilie remembers Jonas and at a picnic that Fabrice was having, she realizes that Jonas was the boy that she used to meet at the pharmacy in her childhood. Jonas, aware that Émilie is taking an interest in him and fearful of hurting his friend Fabrice, avoids Émilie, who realizes it and stops attempting to visit Jonas at the pharmacy.

Meanwhile Madame Cazanave figures out that her daughter thinks a lot about Jonas and make him swear that he won't encourage Émilie to pursue him. This oath is a central point in the story. This oath is one of the things that prevents Jonas from doing what he would have liked to do.

Still, Jonas holds to true to his oath and avoids Émilie.

Meanwhile Émilie unceremoniously ditches Fabrice for Jean-Christophe, who in turn breaks up with Isabelle, breaking both Fabrice's and Isabelle's hearts and causing Simon to profess a disliking for Émilie.

Émilie finally gets a chance to express herself to Jonas when Mahi sends him to buy a book from the local bookshop just when Émilie was substituting for usual storekeeper (Madame Lambert). But Jean-Christophe see them together and blames Jonas for betraying him.

Mad with grief, Jean-Christophe leaves Rio Salado and no one has any news of him for days.

Émilie visits Jonas in his pharmacy to apologize for this turn of events and continues the conversation that they were having at the bookshop before Jean-Christophe showed up. Émilie makes it clear that she loves Jonas and reminds him of their first meeting in their childhood in vivid detail.

Jonas's behaviour leads Émilie to think that he is not being considerate to her because of the way she treated Jean-Christophe and waits for Jonas. She finds silly excuses to visit Jonas in the pharmacy who pretends not to notice her pain.

After a while Émilie stops trying to contact Jonas and asks him to see her if he likes, but Jonas never does.

Later on, Madame Cazenave induces Émilie into marrying Simon. In a last ditch effort, Émilie pays Jonas a discreet visit to request him one last time to accept her love. But Jonas is indecisive and doesn't speak a single word, leading Émilie to leave in despair.

The next morning Émilie sends him back the rose flower that Jonas had pressed into her book in their childhood. Turns out that she valued it that much. This should have made Jonas to make an attempt to put Émilie's mind at ease but he doesn't.

Jonas avoids attending the engagement but can't find a way to safely skip the marriage.

After the marriage, Jonas falls into a period of depression. He flees to Oran and tries to retrace his childhood there. He tries in vain to find Lucette. Finally it takes a prostitute to tell him some sense:

« Ressaisis-toi, bonhomme. Il n'y a qu'un seul dieu sur terre, et c'est toi. Si le monde ne te convient pas, réinventes-en un autre, et ne laisse aucun chagrin te faire descendre de ton nuage. La vie sourit toujours à celui qui sait lui rendre la monnaie de sa pièce. »

Pull yourselves together, chap.. There's only one god here on earth, and that's you. If you don't like the world, make one you like better. Fortune smiles on those who smile on her.'

And Jonas returns to Rio Salado.

A baby boy is born to Simon and Émilie. They name him Michel.

Mahi, who had shown signs of being reclusive again after recovering from his heart attack, dies. Five months later, Algerian war formally begins.

At first, Rio Salado is not much affected by the war. But then José gets murdered right outside André's diner.

André in his grief, picks on Jelloul and accuses him of being involved in the murder due to his Arab ancestry. The police arrests Jelloul.

Jelloul's mother pleads to Jonas to save Jelloul and Jonas attempts to talk to Pépé Rucillio about it wihout success.

Jean-Christophe returns home, after having joined the army and served in the French-Indochina. But Jonas is hurt as he was not invited to the reunion held afterwards.

Jean-Christophe still hadn't forgiven Jonas.

Jonas didn't even know about Jean-Christophe's marriage to Isabelle till it was over.

Jonas comes face to face with the consequences of the ongoing war of the first time when he witnesses a drive-by right that leaves many people dead.

Then one night, a group of people set fire to the Cazanave mansion and kills Simon. Jonas rushes to the place upon seeing the glow of the fire and is devastated to find Simon's body lying on the ground with his throat cut. Émilie forbids Jonas from touching Simon and yells at him get out of her sight.

Émilie leaves Rio Salado with Michel after Simon's funeral.

Jonas makes attempts to find Émilie in Oran every now and then and finally finds her working in a bookshop.

After a few days' hesitancy, Jonas tries to meet Émilie who treats him coldly and wouldn't accept his apologies that were too late.

Jonas doesn't make another attempt to talk to her. Possibly in part due to him becoming complicit in the ongoing civil war. Turns out that Jelloul has joined the rebels. He brings one of his wounded leaders and forces Jonas and Germaine to treat him.

Jonas continues to help Jelloul by clandestinely providing his friends with medicinal supplies. But Krimo, who had become a harki, finds out about this and turns him over to the military.

However, Pépé urged on by Isabelle, pleads on Jonas's behalf and gets him out.

Jonas is surprised that Isabelle helped him despite being in Oran. This leads to a conversation between him and Germaine after which Jonas realizes that she and him have become so distant that Germaine, who had been everything to him, now considered him barely more than a stranger.

Opposition of people who didn't like the idea of Algeria becoming independent manifested in the form of events like the week of barricades and the failed coup of 1961. Despite the reigning chaos, Jonas desperately attempts to find Émilie, but in vain.

The victorious FLN finally marches into Rio Salado and Jelloul repays Jonas's kindness by freeing Jean-Christophe, who had become an active OAS member.

Later on, Dédé finds out that Émilie is in Marseilles, having left Algeria during the exodus that happened around the end of the civil war.

Jonas travels to France and meets her. She seemed to be indifferent, yet becomes soft towards the end of their meeting and tells Jonas that it's no one's fault that things turned out the way they did and that he doesn't owe her anything.

This would be the last time Jonas sees Émilie.

Jonas, disappointed, returns to Algeria.

He wrote her letters, but she never replies.

Time passes and Jonas marries and becomes a father and then a grandfather.

Then he receives news of Émilie's death and decides to visit her tomb in France.

Michel, Simon and Émilie's son, receives Jonas at the airport and they visit Émilie's grave. They have a reunion with many familiar faces. Dédé, Krimo, and Fabrice among them. But Jean-Chrisophe doesn't turn up. Jonas can't help thinking that it's because he's still angry with him.

Michel gives Jonas a box that Émilie had assigned him to deliver to Jonas. Jonas first can't bring himself to open it, but when he does he discovers the letters that he had sent to Émilie. All unopened.

One of the letters was not of Jonas and he opens it. It is a note from Émilie that she wrote only a week before her death saying that she hoped to see Jonas again after their last meeting. She forgives him for tormenting her and asks Jonas to do the same to her. Émilie promises that she will always have a thought for Jonas in the afterlife.

This calms Jonas. Maybe it was the realization that Émilie finally did forgive him.

Jonas prepares to board his flight back to Algeria when Jean-Chrisophe finally makes his appearance. They have a friendly reunion.

Jonas looks back one last time before boarding his flight and imagines all his friends, both living and dead, waving to him.

A nice ending, though with a touch of sadness.

Hadn't known about this event. Was quite surprised. Britain and French were on the same side after all. Over 1000 (mille quatre-vingt-dix-sept) French servicemen were killed. What to say.. Horrors of war…

Notre premier été à Río Salado débuta mal. Le 3 juillet 1940, le pays fut ébranlé par l'opération Catapult qui vit l'escadre britannique « Force H » bombarder les vaisseaux de guerre français amarrés en rade à la base navale de Mers el-Kébir. Trois jours après, ne nous laissant même pas le temps de mesurer l'ampleur de la catastrophe, les avions de Sa Majesté revinrent achever leur travail de sape.

Le neveu de Germaine, cuistot sur le cuirassé Dunkerque, figurait parmi les mille deux cent quatre-vingt-dix-sept marins tués lors de ces raids. Mon oncle, sombrant progressivement dans une sorte d'autisme chronique, refusa de nous accompagner aux funérailles, et nous dûmes partir sans lui, Germaine et moi.

About the author

An Algerian born novelist who writes in French. Yasmina Khadra (ياسمينة خضراء), which means 'green jasmine blossom' ¹², is his pen-name (nom de plume). Real name is Mohammed Moulessehoul (محمد مولسهول). The pen-name was inspired by the name of his wife.

He once served in the Algerian army and may have drawn from his experiences from that time while writing his novels, including this one.

I found out after reading the book that this title is considered the last novel in what is known as the 'Algerian trilogy' of novels by the author.

DONE Discrimination

Those with European-ancestry in French Algeria looked down on the native populace. A fact made totally evident when Jonas sees the place where Jelloul lives.

I thought that I had seen poverty in Jenane Jato; I was wrong. The shanty town where Jelloul and his family lived was beyond anything I had ever imagined. The douar was made up of a dozen squalid hovels on the banks of a dried-up riverbed. A few scrawny goats ambled around. The place smelled so foul I found it difficult to imagine how anyone could spend two days here. When the path petered out, I left the bike on the slopes and helped Jelloul down the hill. The marabout's hill was only a few kilometres from Río Salado, but I could not remember ever having passed this way. People shunned the place, as though it were cursed. Suddenly the simple fact that I was on the far side of the hill terrified me. I was scared something might happen to me, and I knew that if anything did, no one would think to come looking for me here. It was ridiculous, but the fear was all too real. I felt a mortal dread at being in this douar of ramshackle huts pervaded by the stench of rotting flesh.

Je pensais avoir touché le fond de la misère, à Jenane Jato ; je me trompais. La misère du douar où habitait Jelloul et sa famille dépassait les bornes. Le hameau comptait une dizaine de gourbis sordides, au creux d'une rivière morte cernée d'enclos où quelques chèvres squelettiques se morfondaient. L'endroit sentait si mauvais que je n'arrivais pas à croire que des gens puissent y survivre deux jours d'affilée. Incapable de m'aventurer plus loin, je rangeai ma bicyclette sur le bas-côté de la piste et aidai le factotum à mettre pied à terre. La colline aux deux marabouts ne se trouvait qu'à quelques encablures de Río Salado ; pourtant, je ne me souvenais pas d'avoir poussé la randonnée jusque dans les parages. Les gens évitaient de se hasarder par ici. Comme s'il s'agissait d'un territoire maudit. Soudain j'eus peur d'être là, de l'autre côté de la colline ; peur de ne pas m'en sortir indemne, certain que si un accident m'arrivait, personne ne viendrait me chercher là où je n'avais aucune raison de m'aventurer. C'était absurde, mais la crainte était forte, bien réelle. Le hameau, subitement, m'épouvantait. Et cette odeur infernale, si proche de celle des décompositions!

A group of naked children were playing in the dust, their bellies swollen, flies crawling on their faces. Then I realised that it was not simply the stench; it was the drone of the flies, incessant, voracious, filling the foul air like some baleful supplication, like the breath of some demon that lowered over human misery, a sound as old as time itself. At the foot of a low toube wall, a group of old men lay dozing, mouths open, huddled beside a sleeping donkey. A madman, arms raised to the heavens, stood babbling wildly beneath a marabout tree from which hung talismans, coloured ribbons and candle wax. There was no one else: it was as though the douar had been abandoned to feral children and dying men.

Des gamins nus jouaient dans la poussière, le ventre ballonné et les narines assiégées de mouches – oui, c'était ça ; en plus de la puanteur, il y avait le bourdonnement des mouches, vorace, obsessionnel ; il n'arrêtait pas d'engrosser l'air vicié d'une litanie funeste, comme un souffle diabolique planant par-dessus une détresse humaine aussi vieille que le monde et tout aussi affligeante. Au pied d'un muret en toube, à proximité d'une bourrique assoupie, un groupe de vieillards somnolait, la bouche ouverte. Les bras décharnés levés au ciel, un fou s'adressait à un arbre-marabout bigarré de rubans talismaniques et de coulées de cierges… Puis, plus rien ; on aurait dit le hameau déserté par les personnes valides et livré aux mioches faunesques et aux moribonds.

A pack of dogs ran towards me, growling. Jelloul picked up a stone and drove them off. There was silence again. He turned and gave me a strange smile.

Une bande de chiens nous repéra et fonça sur moi en aboyant. Jelloul la repoussa à coups de pierres. Une fois le silence rétabli, il se retourna vers moi et m'adressa un sourire étrange.

‘This is how our people live, Jonas; my people and your people too. Here, nothing ever changes, while you go on living like a prince . . . What's the matter? Why don't you say something? You're shocked; you can't believe it, can you? Maybe now you know why I call myself a dog. Even a dog would not live like this.'

— C'est comme ça que vivent les nôtres, Jonas. Les nôtres qui sont aussi les tiens. Sauf qu'ils n'évoluent pas là où tu te la coules douce… Qu'est-ce que tu as ? Pourquoi ne dis-tu rien ? Tu es choqué ? Tu n'en reviens pas, n'est-ce pas ?… J'espère que tu me comprends maintenant quand je te parle de chien. Même les bêtes n'accepteraient pas de tomber si bas.

Jonas, and probably the 'European part' of Rio Salado didn't even know that people like Jelloul whom they see everyday were living in such conditions.

Dédé, for instance, considers Arabs beneath him.

Peg-Leg at Jenane Jato wonders if Jonas had a French ancestor due to had blue eyes.

Comment ça s'fait que t'as les yeux bleus? Ta mère est française?

Isabelle tells Jonas his eyes being blue isn't enough when she realizes that he is of Arab descent:

Nous ne sommes pas du même monde, monsieur Younes. Et le bleu de tes yeux ne suffit pas.

We are not from the same world, monsieur Younes. And the blue of your eyes is not enough.

Isabelle had been Jonas' friend till she found out that his birth name was Younes. She had even gone far as to declare him her 'fiancée' in one of her childish whims.

When Isabelle confronts him, Jonas is at first confused. Even when she makes it clear that it's because of his name was Younes. Jonas can't understand what difference that would make.

To which Isabelle says 'everything' and that she would never marry an Arab.

This would be his first taste of the discrimination that prevailed in Algeria at the time.

— Menteur ! Je compris, à la sécheresse de son ton et à l'incandescence de son regard, qu'elle m'en voulait à mort. Avant de me claquer les volets de la fenêtre au nez, elle émit un hoquet de mépris et ajouta : — Je suis une Rucillio, as-tu oublié ?… Tu m'imagines mariée à un Arabe ?… Plutôt crever !

Lazy people

Once Jonas hears someone at school saying that Arabs are lazy. This upsets him enough for him to ask Mahi about it.

Mahi says that the French considers Arabs lazy because the latter prefers to enjoy life in a manner different from that of the Europeans.

De retour à la maison, j'étais allé directement trouver mon oncle dans son bureau. — C'est vrai que les Arabes sont paresseux ? Mon oncle était surpris par l'agressivité de mon ton. Il avait reposé le livre qu'il était en train de parcourir et s'était retourné vers moi. Ce qu'il avait lu sur ma figure l'avait attendri. — Viens un peu par ici, mon garçon, m'avait-il dit en m'ouvrant ses bras. — Non… Je veux savoir si c'est vrai. Est-ce que les Arabes sont des paresseux ? Mon oncle s'était pris le menton entre le pouce et l'index en me dévisageant. L'heure était grave ; il me devait des explications. Après avoir réfléchi, il s'était mis en face de moi et m'avait dit : — Nous ne sommes pas paresseux. Nous prenons seulement le temps de vivre. Ce qui n'est pas le cas des Occidentaux. Pour eux, le temps, c'est de l'argent. Pour nous, le temps, ça n'a pas de prix. Un verre de thé suffit à notre bonheur, alors qu'aucun bonheur ne leur suffit. Toute la différence est là, mon garçon.

I suppose 'le temps, ça n'a pas de prix' means means that time is priceless, not that it has no price. Because after all, time is the most valuable thing known to man kind.
