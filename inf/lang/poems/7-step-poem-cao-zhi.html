Poem of seven steps (七步诗)

  • Poem by: Cao Zhi

Mentioned in The romance of the three kingdoms by Luo Guanzhong.

Context

Cao Cao was the effective leader of the state that would eventually become known as Cao Wei during the Three kingdoms period in China.

He was succeeded by his eldest son - Cao Pi.

Cao Pi worried that his brothers would challenge his legitimacy as ruler and summoned them to pay their respects to their new sovereign to see if they posed any threat.

Soon it was the turn of Cao Zhi, Cao Pi’s younger brother. Cao Zhi was an accomplished poet. So Cao Pi said that if Cao Zhi can compose a poem about a given topic within the time taken to walk seven steps, he’ll let him go.

The topic given to Cao Zhi was ‘Brotherhood’. But he was not allowed to use the word ‘brother’.

Cao Zhi successfully crafted a brilliant poem within these constraints that impressed all who heard it - including the King, who let him go free.

This came to be known as the ‘Seven step poem’ (七步诗).

The poem is prominently featured in the historical novel The romance of the three kingdoms by Luo Guanzhong.

Poem

As shown in the English subtitles of a video kindly translated by a person or group people known as ‘Jiang Hu’:

To boil beans, the stalks are burned;
In the kettle, they sobbing yearned:
We two from one same root did grow —
Why the haste to fry me so?

---

Version from C. H. Brewitt Taylor’s translation (from here):

They were boiling beans on a beanstalk fire;
Came a plaintive voice from the pot,
'O why, since we sprang from the selfsame root,
Should you kill me with anger hot?'

---

Version from Robert Moss translation:

Beans asimmer on a beanstalk flame 
From inside the pot expressed their ire:
"Alive we sprouted on a single root —
What's your rush to cook us on the fire?"

Wikipedia entry has pinyin text and other versions.
