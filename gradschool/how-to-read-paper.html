How to read a paper

Notes taken upon reading How to Read a Paper by S. Keshav.

Easy to get overwhelmed before you get a basic idea of the paper if you read all the sections serially.

You need to get the overall idea before delving into the details.

(Also, look at the citations. They almost always lead to more number of interesting papers.)

Three pass approach

Do it in three stages (passes):

  1. General idea about the paper
  2. Grasp the content but not details
  3. Understand details in-depth

Pass 1: General idea

Useful even for paper that aren't of your research interests but might still come useful in future.

Actions

Estimated time: 5-10 minutes

Read:

  • Title
  • Abstract
  • Introduction
  • Section and sub-section heading (ignore content)
  • Conclusion
  • Have a look at the references (see if any of them is familiar)

Expected result

Should be able answer 5 'C's, using which you would be able to decide if the paper is worth analyzing in depth.

  • Category
  • Correctness
  • Context
  • Contributions
  • Clarity

You may choose not to read the paper further for one of the following reasons:

  • Paper doesn't interest you.
  • You don't yet know enough to read and understand the paper.

Category

What kind of a paper is it?

  • Analysis of an existing system?
  • A research prototype description?
  • A measurement paper?

Context

  • What papers are related to this one?
  • What theoretical results was used to analyze the problem?

Correctness

Do the assumptions used look valid?

Because if the assumptions don't hold up, neither would the results.

Contributions

  • What novel results does the paper contribute?
  • What value does the paper add to the research area?

Clarity

Is the paper written well enough to be clearly understandable?

So that you can know how much work you would need to put in to analyze it in depth if needed be.

Pass 2: Content

Estimated time: 1 hour

  • Appropriate for a paper in which you are interested in.
  • Even if the paper doesn't lie in your area of research.

Actions

Read the paper with greater care but ignore inner details like proofs.

  • Note down:
    • key takeaways
    • doubts
    • comments.
  • Look at figures, graphs and illustrations.
    • Are the graph axes properly labelled?
  • Mark relevant reference paper for reading later.

Expected results

  • Grasp the content of the paper.
  • Should be able to explain the main thrust of the paper with supporting evidence.

Pass 3: Details

Estimated time: 4-5 hours (novice) / 1 hour (expert)

  • For a paper that you want to fully understand.
  • Requires great attention to detail.

Actions

  • Try to 'virtually' reimplement the paper and see how it would turn out.
    • Make same assumptions as the paper.
    • Compare your results with stated results.
    • Thus identify the paper's innovations (and errors, if any).
  • Identify and challenge every assumption in every statement.

Expected results

  • Should be able to reconstruct the entire structure of the paper from memory.
  • Identify paper's strong and weak points.
  • Pinpoint:
    • implicit assumptions
    • missing citations to relevant work
    • potential issues with experimental or analytical techniques

What to do when you write a paper

  • We can expect the reviewers (and readers) of a paper to make at most one pass to see if the paper is good enough.
  • Choose coherent section and subsection titles.
  • Make the abstract concise while maintaining a level of comprehensiveness.
  • Avoid too many forward references.
  • Explain acronyms and notations being used beforehand.
